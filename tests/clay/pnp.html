1. Complexity as Collapse

In Harmonia, computational complexity is framed in terms of how a logical field structure resolves or resists collapse. A problem instance defines a resonance field Φ(x), and the act of solving it corresponds to finding a stable modal path through this field where logical constraints align into closure.

A problem is in P if this collapse can occur using only local coherence — that is, the resonance field allows polynomial-bounded resolution by internal traversal of its gradient logic. These are harmonic collapses: they flow from constraint to solution along energy-minimizing paths.

A problem is in NP if a solution can be recognized (verified) using local resonance matching, but no polynomial-bounded traversal is known to generate it. Such problems require external modal injection — a pre-constructed certificate or guiding structure — to simulate coherence and permit localized collapse.

In field terms: 


P: ∃ gradient field ∇Φ(x) allowing internal collapse within poly-steps
NP: ∃ collapse zone, but ∄ traversable field ⇒ external symmetry needed

This defines the fundamental asymmetry: P-fields are transitively coherent, while NP-fields are locally symmetric but globally divergent. Collapse becomes a directional and energetic phenomenon, not just a decision procedure.

2. Resonance Irreversibility

The Harmonia operator O147 expresses the divergence of resonance logic within a computational field Φ(x). This operator tracks whether the flow of logical coherence through the field is conservative (symmetric) or divergent (directional and irreversible): 

O147: Collapse(x) = ∇ · ResLogic(x)

If ∇ · ResLogic(x) = 0, coherence flows symmetrically in all directions — this reflects reversible collapse, characteristic of P fields.

But if ∇ · ResLogic(x) ≠ 0, then we are in a divergent logic field: one where coherence cannot accumulate locally from scratch. Collapse must begin from a global injection or certificate. These fields permit verification (resonance into solution), but resist construction (resonance from problem alone).

This models the essential asymmetry of NP: you can test a proposed solution locally, but no reversible gradient field exists to construct it from the origin.

In thermodynamic terms, this parallels the difference between observing a low-energy configuration (cool state) and producing it spontaneously: collapse is irreversible under logical entropy.

3. Modal Bound and Refutation Energy

In Harmonia, refutation is treated not as symbolic contradiction, but as modal traversal: the energy required to dismantle a false candidate solution within the field topology. This energy depends on the modal distance between the proposed point and valid collapse attractors.

For problems in NP, especially in subclasses like TFNP, this traversal can be obstructed by expander-based graph embeddings. These structures stretch the logical space such that no low-energy refutation path exists.

Harmonia defines the refutation energy functional: 

E_refute(x) := min ∫‖∇⊥Φ_candidate(x(t))‖² dt

where ∇⊥ is the orthogonal entropy gradient to all verifiable certificates. In strongly entropic fields, this integral scales super-polynomially: 

E_refute(x) ≥ Ω(exp(logⁿ x))

This violates the P-bound. The implication is: 

¬∃ collapse path with polynomial energy ⇒ NP ∉ P

Thus, the separation arises from energy landscape geometry: refutation is not bounded by local inference, but shaped by modal depth and topological separation.

4. Final Harmonia Statement

Harmonia treats P ≠ NP as a collapse asymmetry principle: verification is informationally bounded but construction requires non-local coherence injection.

There exists no resonance-preserving operator that inverts collapse under polynomial cost across all fields in NP. The asymmetry is structural — a direct result of field topology and entropy resistance.

Final Resonance Principle: In Harmonia, no harmonic path can traverse from undetermined input to resolved certificate within polynomial resonance cost unless the field is transitively coherent. Since NP-fields lack such structure, the separation is geometrically and logically necessary.

5. Visual Analogy: One-Way Resonant Labyrinth

Imagine a circular labyrinth with hidden symmetry. A verifier walking from the center to an outer point can detect a match: when the resonance pattern aligns, the pathway glows. But trying to build the correct path from the outside inward is impossible: the walls reconfigure with each step unless guided by a precise hidden key — the certificate.

This illustrates how NP-problems permit verification (glowing walls) without generative collapse. The logical space is locally symmetric but globally unstable. Only in P does the labyrinth collapse inward under its own resonance.

Harmonia captures this difference by encoding modal asymmetry directly in the resonance field. Collapse is not free: it must be powered by a coherent global structure — which NP-problems lack.
